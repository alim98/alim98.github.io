1. The Biological Motivation

  • Cellular Progression & Fate
  • Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq)
  • The destructive measurement problem
  • Pseudotime as a Latent Variable
  • RNA Velocity (Spliced vs. Unspliced)

The Temporal Puzzle of Single Cells

In developmental biology, researchers study cellular progression—how cells develop, differentiate, and change state over time. For example, how does a generic stem cell "decide" to become a neuron or a muscle cell? Or how do cells progress through the cell cycle?

To answer these questions, biologists use single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq). This technology counts the number of messenger-RNA (mRNA) molecules inside individual cells. Since mRNA is the "blueprint" actively being read to make proteins, counting these molecules gives us a high-resolution "snapshot" of what the cell is doing at that exact moment (its gene expression state).

The Destructive Measurement Problem

Imagine trying to understand how a caterpillar turns into a butterfly, but every time you take a photograph, the camera flash instantly vaporizes the subject. You would be left with a pile of photographs of different stages, but no video linking them together.

This is exactly what happens in scRNA-seq. The sequencing process physically destroys the cell. You can never observe the same cell twice. You only get isolated, static snapshots.

Pseudotime: Reconstructing the Timeline

Because we lack the actual "video", researchers mathematically arrange these static snapshots along an inferred timeline based on how similar their gene expressions are. This inferred, unobserved temporal axis is called pseudotime. Because it is unobserved and must be estimated purely from the data, mathematically we treat pseudotime as a Latent Variable.

The Game Changer: RNA Velocity

Historically, algorithms only looked at static snapshots to guess the order. But recently, biologists unlocked a way to see a little "arrow" indicating which direction the cell is moving. This metric is called RNA velocity.

How does it work? When a gene is transcribed from DNA, it first produces unspliced (immature) RNA. Shortly after, it is processed into spliced (mature) RNA. By looking at the ratio of unspliced to spliced RNA in our snapshot, we can determine if a gene is currently "turning on" (high unspliced) or "turning off" (low unspliced).

This ratio provides the rate of change of gene expression. In calculus terms, RNA velocity estimates the derivative of gene expression with respect to time.

1b. Deep Dive: RNA Kinetics

  • The Kinetic Rate Equations
  • Splicing ($\beta$) & Degradation ($\gamma$)
  • The Cell Cycle Case Study

The Differential Equation of Life

If we want to be mathematically rigorous about the interactive visualization above, we can express RNA velocity using a system of Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs). The change in spliced RNA ($s$) over time ($t$) is governed by two competing biological forces: the creation of new spliced RNA and the destruction of old spliced RNA.

The RNA Velocity Equation

$$ \frac{ds}{dt} = \beta u - \gamma s $$
  • $s$: Amount of spliced (mature) RNA
  • $u$: Amount of unspliced (immature) RNA
  • $\beta$: The Splicing Rate (how fast $u$ turns into $s$)
  • $\gamma$: The Degradation Rate (how fast $s$ decays and is destroyed)

This equation is the biological bridge to Gaussian Processes. By measuring $u$ and $s$ via sequencing, and computationally estimating the rates $\beta$ and $\gamma$, we obtain a proportional estimate of the mathematical derivative $\frac{ds}{dt}$. This is the directional arrow ($y'$) that we feed into the Derivative GP!

The Case Study: The Cell Cycle

In the paper, the authors apply DGP-LVM to real-world data from Mahdessian et al. (2021) focusing on the Cell Cycle. The cell cycle is the biological process a cell goes through to divide and replicate, moving through four distinct phases: G1 $\rightarrow$ S $\rightarrow$ G2 $\rightarrow$ M.

This is the perfect testing ground for Derivative GPs. Why? Because the cell cycle is a heavily regulated, oscillating biological clock. Genes necessary for DNA synthesis turn ON sharply in the S phase and turn OFF in the G2 phase. By looking at the RNA velocity (the derivative), the DGP-LVM can easily tell if a cell is entering or exiting a phase, allowing it to estimate a highly precise, cyclical pseudotime.

2. Standard GP Recap

  • Distributions over functions
  • Mean and Covariance
  • Notation: $y = f(x) + \epsilon$

A Quick Refresher on Gaussian Processes

A Gaussian Process (GP) is a distribution over functions. Instead of searching for the exact parameters of a rigid equation (like $y = mx + c$), a GP places a probability distribution over an infinite number of possible smooth curves that could fit our data.

A GP is fully specified by a mean function $m(x)$ and a covariance function (or kernel) $K(x, x^T)$.

$$ f(x) \sim \mathcal{GP}(m(x), K(x, x^T)) $$

For a univariate output variable $y$ with independent, identically distributed Gaussian noise, the model is written as:

$$ y_i = f(x_i) + \epsilon_i \quad \text{where} \quad \epsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2) $$ $$ \text{or equivalently, } \quad y_i \sim \mathcal{N}(f(x_i), \sigma^2) $$

The magic of the GP lies in the covariance function $K(x_i, x_j)$. It dictates that if two input points $x_i$ and $x_j$ are close together in time, their outputs $y_i$ and $y_j$ should be highly correlated. For $i \neq j$, $Cov(y_i, y_j) = K(x_i, x_j)$.

3. Derivative GPs (The Math)

  • Differentiation is a Linear Operator
  • Joint Models: $f(x)$ and $f'(x)$
  • The Block Covariance Matrix
  • Step-by-Step Proof

How to Differentiate a Gaussian Process

Here is a beautiful mathematical fact: Differentiation is a linear operator. Because a Gaussian Process is defined entirely by linear combinations of Gaussian variables, applying a linear operator to a GP simply yields another GP!

This means if we have a GP modeling our output $f(x)$, its derivative $f'(x) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$ is also a GP. Even better, they can be modeled jointly using a single, unified block covariance matrix.

$$ \begin{pmatrix} f(x) \\ f'(x) \end{pmatrix} \sim \mathcal{GP} \left( \begin{pmatrix} m_f \\ m_{f'} \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} K & K' \\ K'^T & K'' \end{pmatrix} \right) $$

What are these blocks?

  • $K$: The standard covariance between two regular outputs.
  • $K'$: The cross-covariance between a regular output and a derivative output. It is the first derivative of $K$ with respect to $x$.
  • $K'^T$: The first derivative of $K$ with respect to $x^T$.
  • $K''$: The covariance between two derivative outputs. It is the second-order partial derivative of $K$ with respect to both $x$ and $x^T$.

Interactive Proof: Deriving the Cross-Covariance

Let's prove that the covariance between a point $y_i$ and a derivative point $v_j = \frac{\partial y_j}{\partial x_j}$ is exactly equal to the derivative of their covariance function. Click each step to reveal the math!

Step 1: Start with the Definition of Covariance +
$$ Cov(y_i, v_j) = E [ (y_i - E(y_i)) (v_j - E(v_j)) ] $$ Substitute $v_j$ with its derivative definition $\frac{\partial y_j}{\partial x_j}$.
Step 2: Substitute the Derivative +
$$ = E \left[ (y_i - E(y_i)) \left( \frac{\partial y_j}{\partial x_j} - E\left( \frac{\partial y_j}{\partial x_j} \right) \right) \right] $$
Step 3: Apply the Dominated Convergence Theorem (DCT) +
Under DCT, we can swap the Expectation and the Derivative operators. $E\left( \frac{\partial y_j}{\partial x_j} \right)$ becomes $\frac{\partial}{\partial x_j} E(y_j)$. $$ = E \left[ (y_i - E(y_i)) \left( \frac{\partial y_j}{\partial x_j} - \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j} E(y_j) \right) \right] $$
Step 4: Factor out the Derivative +
$$ = E \left[ (y_i - E(y_i)) \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j} (y_j - E(y_j)) \right] $$ Since $y_i$ is a constant with respect to $x_j$, we can pull the derivative outside the entire expression inside the expectation.
Step 5: Final Result! +
$$ = \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j} E [ (y_i - E(y_i)) (y_j - E(y_j)) ] $$ $$ = \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j} Cov(y_i, y_j) $$ Conclusion: The covariance between an output and a derivative is just the derivative of the original covariance!

4. The Scale Problem

  • Calculus vs. Biology
  • Proportional Estimates
  • Why Standard GPs Fail

When Math Meets Reality

If we had perfect, pure mathematical derivatives, the block covariance matrix above would work perfectly. But we are working with biology. RNA velocity is NOT an exact mathematical derivative.

The Core Issue

RNA velocity is estimated based on the kinetic rates of RNA splicing and degradation. Because of this, it is usually only proportional to the true derivative. It might be 10x larger or 0.1x smaller than the strict mathematical derivative $\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}$.

If you feed a standard derivative GP an observation $y'$ that is on a completely different scale than $y$, the standard math breaks. The model attempts to force a single variance scale onto two completely different magnitudes of data, resulting in massive errors and destroying the latent pseudotime estimation.

To fix this, the authors introduce a brilliant modification: Decoupled Hyperparameters. Instead of using a single GP marginal standard deviation ($\alpha$) for both, they split it:

  • $\alpha$: The standard deviation scaling the original output $y$.
  • $\alpha'$: The standard deviation scaling the derivative output $y'$.

They similarly split the measurement noise into $\sigma$ and $\sigma'$. The length scale $\rho$, however, is shared, as it strictly dictates how fast the inputs (the timeline) change, which is invariant to output scaling.

5. Modified Covariances

  • The Equations
  • Squared Exponential (SE)
  • Matern Class (3/2 & 5/2)

The Scaling Math in Action

Let's look at exactly how the authors modified the famous Squared Exponential (SE) kernel to handle this scaling. Notice where $\alpha$ and $\alpha'$ are placed.

Modified Squared Exponential

1. Standard Covariance $K$ (Output to Output):

$$ K(x_i, x_j) = \color{#2563eb}{\alpha^2} \exp\left(-\frac{(x_i - x_j)^2}{2\rho^2}\right) $$

* Uses only the original output variance $\alpha^2$.

2. Cross-Covariance $K'$ (Output to Derivative):

$$ K'(x_i, x_j) = \color{#9333ea}{\alpha \alpha'} \frac{(x_i - x_j)}{\rho^2} \exp\left(-\frac{(x_i - x_j)^2}{2\rho^2}\right) $$

* Uses the product of both scales $\alpha \alpha'$. Notice the polynomial multiplier $\frac{(x_i - x_j)}{\rho^2}$ that popped out from the first derivative.

3. Derivative Covariance $K''$ (Derivative to Derivative):

$$ K''(x_i, x_j) = \color{#ea580c}{\alpha'^2} \frac{\rho^2 - (x_i - x_j)^2}{\rho^4} \exp\left(-\frac{(x_i - x_j)^2}{2\rho^2}\right) $$

* Uses only the derivative variance $\alpha'^2$. This comes from differentiating twice.

The authors also derived these modifications for the less smooth, more realistic Matern 3/2 and Matern 5/2 kernels. For instance, the Matern 3/2 formulas become quite complex:

$$ K_{M3/2}(x_i, x_j) = \alpha^2 \left(1 + \frac{\sqrt{3}|x_i - x_j|}{\rho}\right) \exp\left(-\frac{\sqrt{3}|x_i - x_j|}{\rho}\right) $$
$$ K'_{M3/2}(x_i, x_j) = \alpha \alpha' \frac{3(x_i - x_j)}{\rho^2} \exp\left(-\frac{\sqrt{3}|x_i - x_j|}{\rho}\right) $$

6. Multidimensional Outputs

  • Multiple Genes = Multiple Dimensions
  • Correlated Outputs
  • Cholesky Factor $L$

Handling Multiple Genes at Once

A single cell doesn't just have one gene; it has thousands. If we modeled each gene with a completely separate GP, we would throw away massive amounts of biological information about how genes interact with each other (gene regulatory networks).

DGP-LVM solves this using Multi-output GPs. The idea is to build a structure that allows information sharing across dimensions.

  1. First, we set up $D$ independent univariate GPs $f_d(x)$, each with its own set of scaling hyperparameters $(\rho_d, \alpha_d, \alpha'_d, \sigma_d, \sigma'_d)$. Note that each gene is allowed to have a different smoothness ($\rho$) and scale ($\alpha$).
  2. We then mathematically intertwine them using a $D \times D$ across-dimension correlation matrix $C$.
  3. We take the Cholesky decomposition of $C$ to get a lower-triangular matrix $L$ (where $C = L L^T$).

The final, correlated GP predictions $\tilde{f}$ are obtained by multiplying the independent GPs by the Cholesky factor:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} \tilde{f}_1(x_i) \\ \dots \\ \tilde{f}_D(x_i) \end{pmatrix} = L \times \begin{pmatrix} f_1(x_i) \\ \dots \\ f_D(x_i) \end{pmatrix} $$

This exact same matrix multiplication is applied to the derivative outputs $f'_d(x_i)$ to get $\tilde{f}'_d(x_i)$.

7. Latent Inputs (Pseudotime)

  • Unobserved $x$
  • Prior Measurements $\tilde{x}$
  • Latent GPs

Finding the Missing Timeline

Up until now, we have assumed we know the input $x$ (time). But in scRNA-seq, $x$ is pseudotime, which is entirely unobserved! This is why it's a Latent Variable Model (LVM).

We do, however, have a noisy hint. In biological experiments, we know the "cell capture time" or "experimental time"—the literal hour the cell was frozen. This experimental time $\tilde{x}$ is a noisy, rough proxy for true developmental pseudotime $x$.

We model this mathematically as a prior distribution, assuming the noisy observation $\tilde{x}$ is drawn from a Gaussian centered around the true latent time $x$, with a known measurement error $s$:

$$ \tilde{x}_i \sim \mathcal{N}(x_i, s^2) $$

By defining $x$ as a parameter with this prior, the GP isn't just fitting a curve to data points; it is actively moving the data points horizontally along the x-axis to find the sequence that yields the most probabilistically likely smooth curves across all $D$ gene dimensions simultaneously!

8. The Full DGP-LVM Model

  • Factorized Probability
  • Bayesian Inference via HMC
  • Empirical Success

Putting It All Together

The entire DGP-LVM framework can be expressed as a massive joint probability density. By marginalizing out the actual GP functions $f$ and $f'$ (a superpower of Gaussian math), we are left with a probability density that factorizes over dimensions $d$:

$$ p(y, y', x, \theta) = \prod_{d=1}^D p(y_d | x, \theta_d) p(y'_d | x, \theta_d) p(x) p(\theta_d) $$

Where $\theta$ represents all our customized hyperparameters. We then use Bayes' rule and computationally heavy Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) via Stan to sample from the posterior distribution $p(x, \theta | y, y')$. This gives us not just a single best guess for the pseudotime $x$, but a full distribution representing our uncertainty.

Did the Scaling Modification Work?

Yes, drastically. In the paper's simulation studies, models that included derivative information but failed to use the scaling modifications ($\alpha, \alpha'$) performed abysmally—often worse than models that ignored derivatives entirely.

By enabling the scaled derivatives, DGP-LVM reduced the Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) of the latent input estimations by over 30% compared to standard GPs. In a real-world cell-cycle case study, the model successfully adjusted experimental cell hours into tight, confident posterior pseudotime trajectories.
